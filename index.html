Governance

Governance that makes security hold

Governance is not built to satisfy a model. It is built so security holds in real life.

Threat pressure keeps rising. Security only helps when rules are clear, procedures are usable, ownership is explicit, escalation is known, and risk is expressed in business terms. Otherwise it stays theoretical.

A good solid governance is the base for compliance as well. It all starts and ends with policies - the policies that exist and are managed, the policies that do not exist and are needed, and the policies that exist and are not managed.

Policy is not literature. It is the rule-set - what must be done, what must not. Short, clear, owned. Procedures take that rule-set and make it usable in daily work. Not a reference document. A working tool.

Frameworks are beacons. ISO 27001, NIS2, NIST CSF give direction - they also expose the holes. The real test is not whether a gap was identified. It is whether it moves: owned, accepted, mitigated, or closed. Findings without movement are just inventory.

Ownership Structure

Who has the mandate to decide. Who owns execution. Who escalates, to whom, within what timeframe. Without that, security is not managed - it runs on assumption. Assumed ownership is already a failure.

Awareness & Measurement

Not content pushed at employees. Management needs awareness of the program itself - its reach, its weak points, whether it is working. Each cycle is measured. The next round is built on what the last one revealed.

Trust Chain

Governance does not stop at the organization’s edge. Vendors, suppliers, and SBOM exposure are part of the same trust chain. What we accept from them is what we pass to customers.

Risk Management

Risk that informs decisions

Risk management is not there to decorate governance with a register. It exists to put a decision on the table.

I approach that chain in practical terms: identify and assess through credible scenarios, identify in business context and assign ownership where the consequence sits, communicate and consult in business language, and keep every treated or accepted risk under review. A risk register is a tool. The product is a decision.

01  Discovery & Assessment

Scenario-based.

Likelihood and impact expressed in business terms: operational disruption, financial damage, legal or regulatory exposure, customer impact, management attention, and cost of delay.

02  Ownership

Business context.

Once a risk is flagged and evaluated, it has an owner. The IT people are not the default owners, even if the risk was found in a technical system. The owner is the business owner carrying the consequence and accountable for the residual exposure.

03  Communication & Review

We do not withhold risk from management or the board. We bring it forward in language that supports a real discussion: exposure, plausible damage, options, required decision, residual risk, and timeline. Then it is tracked, treated, accepted, escalated where needed, and reviewed on a defined cycle.

If risk has no stated value, reporting has no business value. Management does not need more cyber vocabulary. It needs the plausible damage, the exposure, the decision on the table, and the cost of delay. Risk translated into business terms enables decisions. Everything else is information theatre.

— A VPN vulnerability is not a risk. Malicious access to the ERP via an unpatched remote connection is.

— Unrotated API keys are not a risk. Breached access to the finance database via a compromised credential is.

Part of enterprise risk. IT risk does not live on a cloud of its own. It is part of the organization’s risk picture and should be managed there. The discipline is the same: understand the context, assess the scenario, decide on treatment, accept what is consciously retained, communicate clearly, and review on purpose.

Compliance & Audit

The path to compliance

The value of compliance is not only in passing audit, but in building a stronger and more reliable operating baseline. Implementation can land anywhere between compliance theater and a control model so rigid that normal business starts working around it. What matters here is a practical, pragmatic approach.

Once the scope is defined, the work is to map the gaps between what is happening today and what it should look like - building a managed set of policies aligned with requirements, reflected in practical procedures, and demonstrable through evidence. When something truly operates, it leaves evidence behind it.

Audits do not usually fail because of the clause itself. They fail in the seams - the policy says one thing, the procedure says another, ownership is unclear, evidence is partial, and reporting starts only when the audit is near. That is where compliance becomes practical, defensible, and valuable.
